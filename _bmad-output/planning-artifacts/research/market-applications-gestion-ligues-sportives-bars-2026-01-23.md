### Pain Points and Challenges

**Tournament Coordination Issues:**
Players historically struggle with not knowing their bracket position, when they were scheduled to play, or who their opponents were. Prior tournament software attempts failed to gain traction despite offering features like player notifications and login capabilities. Manual tournament organization methods—using paper brackets, handwritten entries, and hat draws—were "very tiresome to put together and maintain."

**Scoring & Dispute Prevention:**
Manual scoring creates disputes and confusion during games, especially in bar settings with multiple simultaneous matches. Traditional beer pong tables lack the ability to automate scoring, making real-time updates difficult. Organizers need reliable, transparent score tracking systems.

**Venue Operations:**
Bars struggle to manage high-volume games efficiently, create consistent scoring systems, and encourage repeat business through competitive engagement. The lack of automation in tournament management creates operational bottlenecks during peak periods.

**Platform Limitations:**
Early solutions relied on spreadsheet-based approaches (Excel or Google Sheets) that were difficult to maintain and customize. The absence of unified systems forces organizers to juggle multiple tools for registration, scheduling, payments, and communication.

### Decision-Making Processes

When groups use sports apps to make decisions about activities, users rely on social similarity and preference information to reach consensus. Group decision support systems minimize preference queries by leveraging social network data—groups with high internal social similarity require fewer queries and less user communication to reach decisions.

Social influence plays a critical role in users' decisions to continue using sports apps through three mechanisms: subjective norms (peer pressure), social image (reputation concerns), and social identity (group belonging). These factors drive perceived usefulness and engagement, which then determine whether users maintain participation.

Self-quantification features influence how users make behavioral decisions about activity selection and participation goals. When users have explicit goals, quantification enhances perceived certainty and enjoyment, improving both participation outcomes and novelty-seeking behavior.

Tournament organizers prioritize platforms that eliminate manual work and provide unified systems handling registration, scheduling, payments, and communication. They seek partners—not just products—that match their specific event scale and requirements, supported by personalized onboarding and demos.
